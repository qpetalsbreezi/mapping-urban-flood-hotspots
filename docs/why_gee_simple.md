# Why Google Earth Engine? Simple Answer

## The Problem

You need to download satellite data for **18 flood events** (9 in Raleigh, 9 in Houston).

---

## Option 1: Just Use Copernicus Browser (Manual)

**What you'd do:**
- Click through Copernicus Browser 18 times
- Manually search and download each event
- Download about 36 files total (Sentinel-1 and Sentinel-2 for each event)
- Wait for ~50-60 GB of data to download
- Organize all the files yourself

**Time needed:** About 4-6 hours of clicking and waiting

**It works, but it's:**
- ❌ Repetitive and boring
- ❌ Easy to make mistakes
- ❌ Time-consuming

---

## Option 2: Use Google Earth Engine (Automated)

**What you'd do:**
- Write one Python script that does everything
- Run the script once
- Wait for it to finish automatically

**Time needed:** About 30 minutes to write the script, then automatic

**It's:**
- ✅ Faster
- ✅ Can process in the cloud (don't need to download huge files)
- ✅ Less error-prone
- ✅ More professional for science fair

---

## The Bottom Line

**Do you NEED GEE?** No.  
**Should you USE GEE?** Yes, if you want to save time and do a better job.

**For a science fair project**, using automated tools (like GEE) is actually a **plus** because it shows you know how to use professional research tools.

---

## What Should You Do?

**Start with Copernicus Browser** to:
- Learn how the data looks
- Download 1-2 test events to understand the data

**Then apply for GEE** to:
- Download the remaining events quickly
- Do your analysis more efficiently

**Result:** You save time, make fewer mistakes, and your project looks more professional.

---

That's it! GEE is just a tool to make your life easier.
