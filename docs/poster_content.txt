## 0. Contributions and abstract hooks

Use this section when drafting the abstract and the "intellectual merit / broader impacts" portions of the project description.

### Project goal

To develop a remote-sensing–based approach that integrates satellite imagery and ground observations to identify and predict recurring urban flood hotspots, enabling data-driven strategies for flood mitigation, infrastructure planning, and emergency response in urban environments.

### Problem and motivation

- Flash floods are among the most dangerous and unpredictable urban natural hazards, causing severe property damage, traffic disruption, and loss of life. As climate change increases the intensity of extreme rainfall, cities are becoming more vulnerable to sudden, localized flooding.
- Urban flood risk is growing but hard to map. Heavier rainstorms, more pavement, and aging drainage systems are making cities flood more often, especially in streets and neighborhoods far from large rivers.
- Traditional flood maps miss many real-world floods. FEMA and similar maps focus on riverine flooding and are updated slowly; they often do not show street-level and pluvial flooding that residents actually experience.
- Mapping urban floods is technically challenging. Dense infrastructure causes radar shadowing and distortion in satellite images; paved surfaces (roads, rooftops) can resemble floodwater in both radar and optical data. Urban floods also develop and recede quickly, making them hard to capture within standard satellite revisit cycles.
- Data and models are limited in many places. Detailed hydrologic models require dense gauge networks, detailed terrain/pipe data, and lots of computing power, which many communities do not have.
- SAR + ground integration can help. Prior work shows that SAR can penetrate clouds and detect surface water even in dense urban areas, and that integrating satellite data with ground observations improves flood detection accuracy in complex environments. This project applies that idea to build recurring flood hotspot maps from actual storm events.

### One-paragraph method summary (for abstract)

We build flood hotspot maps for Raleigh, NC and Houston, TX by combining historical flood reports, stream gauge data, and satellite radar imagery. First, we filter NOAA Storm Events by county, location, and narrative, then require high water levels at USGS gauges to select significant urban flood events. For each event, we match pre‑ and post‑storm Sentinel‑1 SAR images with the same orbit, compute backscatter change, and apply tuned thresholds together with urban land‑cover masks and permanent‑water masks to detect flooded pixels. We then aggregate the per‑event flood masks across all qualifying events to produce urban flood frequency maps, and we validate the detections against NOAA point reports and, for Hurricane Harvey, high‑resolution aerial imagery. This produces flood heatmaps based on actual storms that can be compared across cities and reused for planning.

### Main contributions (intellectual merit)

- Flood heatmaps from real storms, not synthetic scenarios  Uses actual historical storm events (NOAA + USGS) to build hotspot maps, instead of relying on hypothetical or synthetic storms.

- Captures more than just riverine flooding  Focuses on urban flooding in streets, neighborhoods, and built-up areas (via WorldCover + NLCD), not only along rivers and main channels.

- Works even where gauge data are sparse  Once calibrated with a handful of well-observed events, the SAR-based method can be applied in places with limited or no long-term stream gauge records.

- Robust to changing storm patterns  Because the method aggregates many events and (optionally) can weight recent ones more heavily than older ones, it can adapt as flood patterns shift with climate and land-use change.

### Broader impacts

- Can be combined with other flooding data  Flood hotspot maps can be overlaid with insurance claims, 911 calls, road closure data, and community reports to create a more comprehensive picture of flood risk.

- Useful for mitigation planning and emergency management  Hotspots can help cities prioritize infrastructure upgrades, green infrastructure, and maintenance, and can inform where to focus warnings, evacuation routes, and emergency alerts.

- Equity and environmental justice analysis  Overlay flood hotspots with census and social vulnerability data to identify neighborhoods that flood repeatedly but have fewer resources, supporting more equitable investments.

- Critical infrastructure and lifeline protection  Intersect hotspots with hospitals, schools, fire stations, power and water facilities, and key roads/bridges to prioritize hardening and maintain access during floods.

### Limitations and assumptions

- Urban-only flood mapping. The algorithm is designed to map flooding in built‑up and impervious areas; it intentionally ignores flooding in non‑urban land covers, so it is not a full floodplain map.
- Subset of storms and data. Only events with both credible NOAA reports and usable Sentinel‑1 pairs are included, so the hotspot map summarizes major, well‑observed floods rather than every minor event.
- Threshold tuning and data quality. Results depend on SAR threshold settings and on the completeness and accuracy of NOAA reports and USGS gauge records; missed reports or sensor issues can affect the hotspot pattern.
- No full hydraulic modeling. The method detects where water likely was, but it does not simulate water depth or velocity and should complement—not replace—detailed hydraulic studies where those are available.

### Future work and extensions

- Apply the same pipeline to additional cities with different climates and drainage systems to test how well it generalizes.
- Experiment with adaptive thresholds and explicit event weighting (giving more weight to recent storms) to better capture evolving flood risk under climate and land‑use change.
- Integrate additional ground data sources (911 calls, road closure logs, insurance claims, community science reports) for stronger validation and richer hotspot interpretation.
- Package the workflow and outputs into a simple web map or decision‑support tool that planners and emergency managers can use without writing code.

### Plain-language 2–3 sentence summary

Heavy rainstorms are flooding city streets and neighborhoods more often, but many existing maps still focus only on big rivers. In this project, we use satellite radar images from real storms, combined with local gauges and flood reports, to build maps that show where urban areas have flooded again and again in Raleigh and Houston. These "flood heatmaps" can help communities see hidden patterns of risk and decide where to focus fixes and emergency planning.

## 7. Algorithm / filtering steps (short)

You can list these as "Processing steps" or "Detection pipeline":

1. Pre-event / post-event S1: Same orbit; pre within ~30 days, post within ~2 days.
2. Change detection: dB difference (post − pre); negative = possible flood.
3. Threshold: Fixed −1.8 dB (VV+VH) or −2.0 dB (VV only); optional adaptive percentile.
4. Urban mask: WorldCover built-up or NLCD impervious ≥20%.
5. Exclude permanent water: JRC ≥50% occurrence.
6. Speckle filter: 90 m smoothing; keep only connected areas ≥5 pixels.

This shows that "flood" is not raw SAR drop but a defined, repeatable product.
