Urban flash flooding is one of the most dangerous hazards, yet many flood maps focus on rivers and do not capture repeated street-level flooding. As storms become more intense and cities become more paved, communities need tools to predict where flooding occurs in real events.
A data-driven system was designed and validated to integrate satellite radar imagery and ground data to identify recurring urban flood hotspots in Raleigh, NC and Houston, TX. Flood events (2010–2025) were collected from the NOAA Storm Events database and filtered using location criteria and USGS stream gauge measurements. This reduced 327 records to 24 floods, of which 16 (7 Raleigh, 9 Houston) had pre- and post-storm satellite radar (SAR) image pairs.
Flooding was mapped from pre- and post-storm SAR image changes, with noise reduction, removal of permanent water bodies, and a focus on built-up areas. These maps were then aggregated into multi-event flood frequency hotspots.
In Raleigh, hotspots were distributed across the urban area, with some concentration along Crabtree Creek, while in Houston they were broader near Buffalo Bayou and low-lying areas. To evaluate accuracy, detected flood areas were compared with NOAA flood reports; 55% fell within 500 meters and 89% within 1000 meters of detected flood areas. For Hurricane Harvey (2017) in Houston, mapped flood areas showed strong agreement with independent aerial imagery.
These results demonstrate that combining real storm events with radar data can identify consistent, street-level flooding patterns beyond traditional river-based maps and provide actionable information for infrastructure planning and emergency response.
